# Publication Review of Anticipatory Active-Aerodynamic Mode Prediction in Formula 1 Racing

This review evaluates the uploaded manuscript, *Anticipatory Active-Aerodynamic Mode Prediction in Formula 1 Racing: A Cross-Circuit Multi-Model Benchmark with Interpretability Analysis*. My overall verdict is that the paper is **not ready for IEEE conference submission in its current form**. The strongest reasons are not stylistic; they are foundational. The paper’s target label is defined as a deterministic speed-and-gear rule rather than a measured aerodynamic state, that rule is presented as if it were mandated by the 2026 regulations even though the current FIA technical and sporting regulations describe driver-adjustable bodywork through **Corner Mode / Straight Mode** and **circuit-specific activation zones**, not a universal `speed ≥ 240 km/h and gear ≥ 6` trigger, and the experiments mix 2025 and 2026 telemetry despite a materially changed 2026 vehicle/aero package. Those three points alone would trigger serious technical objections from experienced reviewers. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn23view0turn9view1turn9view4turn25view3turn3news0

The manuscript is well written and more polished than the average student paper. It also contains one genuinely interesting empirical observation: on this task, a simple instantaneous model outperforms deeper sequence models under leave-one-circuit-out evaluation. However, because the task is largely a rule-derived threshold-forecasting problem rather than a measured control-state prediction problem, that result is much less surprising than the paper implies. Recent work in time-series benchmarking has shown that a meaningful fraction of time-series classification tasks are weakly temporal or effectively tabular, and broader tabular-learning literature likewise shows that simple or tree-based methods often remain very competitive against deep models on medium-sized structured datasets. The paper should therefore be reframed as a **proxy-label forecasting benchmark and benchmarking cautionary tale**, not as a validated predictive-control study for real 2026 active aero. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn20academia0turn21academia0turn24academia1

## Executive assessment

At a high level, the paper has a promising narrative arc but weak scientific footing. The narrative is clear: formulate a one-second-ahead prediction problem, evaluate multiple model families across circuits, show that simple instantaneous baselines win, and use attribution analysis to diagnose a recurrent failure case. That is a reasonable conference story. The difficulty is that the paper currently overstates what it has actually demonstrated. It does **not** demonstrate real active-aero mode prediction from measured actuator states; it demonstrates forecasting of a manually constructed label. It does **not** cleanly isolate cross-circuit generalization; it conflates circuit, season, driver/team, and car-generation effects. It does **not** fully establish the mechanistic cause of the GRU failure; it proposes a plausible explanation supported by attribution, but not by targeted counterfactual experiments. fileciteturn0file0

If I were handling this paper as a senior IEEE reviewer, I would categorize the paper as a **clear reject as submitted**, but with salvage potential **only if** the authors are willing to substantially reframe the work, correct the regulatory and physics grounding, rebuild the evaluation protocol, and add several must-have experiments. The present version is closer to an advanced project report with strong storytelling than to an acceptance-ready conference paper. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn23view0turn9view1turn25view3

The paper also contains several **internal consistency problems** that materially reduce trust. The most serious is that the paper says the label is `y = 1[v ≥ 240 km/h ∧ gear ≥ 6]`, reports Monaco as having **13.2% X-Mode frames**, and later says Monaco’s **maximum speed is ~215 km/h**. Those three statements cannot all be true simultaneously. Either the Monaco speed statement is wrong, the label construction is misstated, or the reported X-Mode percentage is wrong. Any one of those possibilities is serious; the coexistence of all three is reviewer-visible and damaging. fileciteturn0file0

A second internal inconsistency concerns the feature inventory. The paper says raw telemetry provides ten measurements and that appending kinetic energy and longitudinal force yields **12 features**, which matches the logistic-regression parameter count of 13 with intercept. But later tables and captions separately use or report **Acceleration**, **Longitudinal_Force**, and **gear-shift rate**, implying either undocumented engineered features or inconsistent naming. Reproducibility is therefore weaker than the paper suggests. fileciteturn0file0

## Section-by-section publication review

**Title.** The title is clear, topical, and attractive, but it currently overclaims the scientific object being predicted. The manuscript is not actually predicting a measured active-aerodynamic mode; it is predicting a proxy label constructed from telemetry and presented as regulation-derived. The words “benchmark” and “interpretability analysis” are also slightly inflated: the model coverage is broad but still missing key tabular and domain-generalization baselines, and the interpretability component is essentially a narrow case study on Monaco for two deep models. A more accurate IEEE-grade title would be: **“One-Second-Ahead Forecasting of a Telemetry-Derived Aero-State Proxy in Formula 1: A Leave-One-Circuit-Out Benchmark”** or, if the authors validate the regulation logic, **“Cross-Circuit Forecasting of Driver-Adjustable Bodywork State from Formula 1 Telemetry.”** fileciteturn0file0

**Abstract.** The abstract is dense, technically literate, and unusually honest in one respect: it explicitly admits that the label is a deterministic function of instantaneous inputs and that the ML task is essentially threshold forecasting. That honesty is a strength. The weakness is that the abstract still frames the study as if it were grounded in real 2026 active-aero actuation. It also includes too many numbers, too many model names, and too much interpretation for a conference abstract. The missing element is the most important one: a direct statement that the target is a **proxy label** and not a measured actuator state. The abstract also relies on a regulatory premise that is not aligned with the current official 2026 sporting/technical documents, which describe driver-adjustable bodywork through activation zones and straight/corner modes. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn23view0turn9view1turn9view4

A stronger IEEE-quality abstract would read as follows:

> We study one-second-ahead forecasting of a telemetry-derived aero-state proxy in Formula 1 under cross-circuit domain shift. Using FastF1 telemetry from four dry race sessions, we construct a binary proxy label intended to approximate straight-versus-corner aerodynamic operation and evaluate instantaneous and sequence-based models under a leave-one-circuit-out protocol. Across 159,712 frames, a single-frame logistic-regression baseline achieves the best mean AUC-ROC of 0.963 ± 0.021, outperforming the tested LSTM, GRU, TCN, CNN, and Transformer variants. This result indicates that, for the proxy task studied here, most predictive signal resides in the current sensor state rather than in long temporal history. Attribution analysis suggests that recurrent models can over-emphasize circuit-specific dynamic cues and fail under severe domain shift, as illustrated by a Monaco test fold. The study therefore contributes a cross-circuit benchmark and a cautionary result: when the target depends strongly on instantaneous, domain-invariant variables, simple baselines can dominate heavier temporal models. Because the target is derived rather than directly measured, the findings should be interpreted as benchmark evidence rather than as deployment-ready validation of an active-aerodynamic controller.

**Introduction.** The introduction is readable and well paced, but the central premise is the paper’s biggest technical vulnerability. The manuscript says that the 2026 regulations mandate active aerodynamic switching based on **speed ≥ 240 km/h and gear ≥ 6** and builds the entire label generation around that rule. The current FIA regulations instead define driver-adjustable bodywork in terms of **Corner Mode / Straight Mode**, **full or partial activation**, and **circuit-specific activation zones** communicated before each competition; the technical regulations also specify a maximum transition time of **400 ms** for the front and rear wing mechanisms. This means the manuscript’s core problem formulation is either based on an incorrect regulatory interpretation or on an unstated private/team-level control heuristic that has been incorrectly attributed to FIA regulations. That is a major acceptance threat because it undermines the validity of the target itself. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn23view0turn9view1turn9view4

The latency argument is also under-supported. The paper claims a 100–300 ms mechanical/hydraulic actuation delay and uses that to justify 1 s anticipation, but the cited support is an old aerodynamics book rather than a 2026 mechanism-specific source, while the current regulations specify a maximum transition time of 400 ms. A one-second horizon may still be reasonable, but it is not justified rigorously; it could also be unnecessarily early and create avoidable downforce loss before the proper activation zone. The introduction should justify horizon selection with a control tradeoff study, not a rhetorical example. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn23view0turn23view1

**Related work.** The related-work section is organized and readable, but it is thinner and less current than the paper needs. It covers anomaly detection, RNNs, TCNs, Transformers, and domain adaptation, yet it misses several references that are directly relevant to the manuscript’s final message. Most importantly, it omits literature showing that temporal order may contribute little on some time-series classification tasks and literature showing that classical or tabular models can outperform deeper architectures on structured data. Those omitted works would substantially strengthen — and contextualize — the paper’s main empirical result. It also omits stronger recent time-series backbones and recent time-series OOD/domain-generalization literature. As written, the related-work section makes the surprise of the authors’ result seem larger than it is. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn20academia0turn21academia0turn24academia1turn18academia1turn18academia0turn19academia0turn20academia1

A second weakness is comparison framing. Table I reduces prior work to a simplistic checklist and risks appearing strawman-like. Several of the “gaps” are defined in a way that makes the current paper uniquely satisfy them by construction. Reviewers often react badly to that style. A better approach is to define a narrower gap: **cross-domain forecasting of telemetry-derived control proxies in motorsport under grouped evaluation**, then show exactly how prior work differs. fileciteturn0file0

**Methodology and proposed framework.** The methodology section is ambitious and generally understandable, but it has multiple technical and reproducibility problems. The first is the previously noted target issue. The second is the use of telemetry from **Monaco 2025, Monza 2025, Silverstone 2025, and Suzuka 2026** while framing the study as a 2026 active-aero problem. Because the 2026 cars are materially different in aero concept, energy deployment, and package design, the setup does not isolate cross-circuit generalization; it mixes **circuit shift** with **season/car-generation shift**. That means the held-out domain is actually closer to *circuit × season × car package*, not “circuit” alone. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn3news0turn9view1turn23view0turn25view3

The third methodological problem is feature design. The paper includes absolute GPS coordinates, elevation delta, and cumulative lap distance while claiming to study cross-circuit generalization. Those variables are highly domain-specific and can directly encode circuit identity. If deep models overfit circuit-specific trajectories, that may be partly because the feature set itself invites memorization. The paper should therefore include a **feature-ablation study** removing absolute position features, a version with **normalized curvilinear track coordinates**, and a version using only variables plausibly available and meaningful for real control. Without those ablations, the causal claim that “temporal context hurts” is incomplete; the real story may be “temporal models overfit because the feature set leaks domain identity.” fileciteturn0file0

The fourth issue is physics correctness. The manuscript sets `m = 798 kg` and explicitly says this is the **2026 FIA minimum car mass**, but the current 2026 technical regulations specify a different minimum-mass basis. Even apart from that mismatch, using a fixed mass across two seasons and entire race sessions is a crude simplification for kinetic-energy and longitudinal-force construction. Because those features are deterministic transforms of already observed variables under a fixed mass assumption, the “physics-derived” characterization is also somewhat overstated; they add scaling and nonlinearity, not genuinely new physical observables. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn25view3

The fifth issue is interpretability design. Integrated Gradients are computed with a **zero baseline**. For racing telemetry, a zero vector is not a natural counterfactual state; it is an unphysical reference. Since IG is baseline-sensitive, the manuscript should justify the baseline choice and compare it against a more meaningful baseline such as per-feature training mean, idle-straight baseline, or low-speed neutral state. The paper also says attention weights were extracted, but no meaningful attention analysis is actually presented. The interpretability section therefore promises more than the results deliver. fileciteturn0file0

**Experimental setup.** The reported setup is more detailed than average, but still not reproducible enough. The largest issue is data splitting. In Phase 1, the current-state classifiers are trained on an **80/20 stratified split** of dense telemetry frames from the same session. In sequential data, randomized frame-level splits almost always leak near-duplicate neighborhoods across train and test, making the near-perfect AUC unsurprising and not publication-worthy. In Phase 2, the paper uses sliding windows of length 50 with horizon 10, but the validation split within the three training circuits is described as **stratified by class label**, not grouped by lap, stint, or contiguous segment. Overlapping windows likely appear in both train and validation subsets. This can substantially inflate performance and stabilize early stopping unjustifiably. A serious review committee would expect **grouped or blocked splits at the lap or stint level**. fileciteturn0file0

The hyperparameter-selection protocol is also problematic. The paper says a coarse grid search on a **validation split of the Monza circuit** was done before final LOCO evaluation to prevent test-circuit leakage. That statement is unconvincing. If Monza is one of the four domains later used as a held-out test fold, then using Monza for global hyperparameter selection *is* a form of target-domain exposure. The correct solution is **nested LOCO** or a strictly training-fold-only tuning scheme. This issue alone will draw reviewer criticism. fileciteturn0file0

The setup also omits several critical details: exact driver/team identity per circuit, whether pit laps / safety car / VSC / yellow-flag laps were excluded, whether windows crossing lap boundaries were removed, how normalization was computed per fold, what decision threshold was used for F1 reporting, and what unit was resampled for bootstrap significance tests. These are not minor omissions; they directly affect validity. Table IV further hurts reproducibility by listing alternative CPUs and operating systems rather than the one actual system used. fileciteturn0file0

**Results and discussion.** The Phase 2 result that LR-instant wins at 1 s horizon is clearly reported and is the strongest part of the paper. But the interpretation is repeatedly stronger than the evidence. The paper says Random Forest AUC = 1.000 indicates **perfect linear separability**, which is technically incorrect; a random forest is nonlinear. It discusses a “threshold-rule baseline” but never actually reports that baseline experimentally. It treats the transition-window degradation as an “irreducible information-theoretic limit,” yet Table XI shows drops ranging from **−0.045 to −0.406**, which is not the kind of tight consistency that justifies a strong irreducibility claim. That claim would require either formal lower-bound reasoning or stronger empirical elimination of alternative explanations. fileciteturn0file0

The GRU Monaco diagnosis is plausible but under-proven. The attribution evidence supports the idea that the GRU relies more on dynamic trajectory-like signals than the Transformer does, but it does not prove that “circuit speed memorization” is the mechanism. A reviewer will ask for one or more targeted falsification tests: retrain with per-circuit speed normalization, remove GPS/lap-distance features, substitute relative-speed features, or probe hidden states directly. Without such experiments, the conclusion should be softened from “identifying a previously undescribed failure mode” to “suggesting a plausible failure mechanism.” fileciteturn0file0

The Phase 1 results should also be downweighted. On a label explicitly defined from current-frame features, perfect current-state classification contributes little academically. The anomaly-detection phase is coherent, but not well integrated with the main cross-circuit prediction problem. It currently reads like an auxiliary mini-paper appended to the main one. In a conference review, that often harms rather than helps because it spreads the contribution across too many partial stories. The paper would be stronger if Phase 1 were reduced to a short exploratory appendix or removed entirely. fileciteturn0file0

**Conclusion.** The conclusion is fluent and memorable, but too strong. It moves from benchmark findings to control prescriptions — for example, that deployment-ready systems should rely on instantaneous sensor state and incorporate hysteresis — without simulator evidence, hardware profiling, or real actuation labels. It also generalizes from a small four-domain proxy study to “motorsport, automotive, and industrial domains,” which is far too broad. The conclusion should summarize what was genuinely shown and stop there. A defensible conclusion would emphasize that, on this telemetry-derived proxy task and under the reported evaluation, simple instantaneous baselines were stronger than the tested temporal deep models. fileciteturn0file0

## Citation, references, figures, and tables audit

Overall, the citation practice is **below acceptance-ready level for a paper making strong engineering and regulatory claims**. The paper contains some appropriate foundational citations, but a nontrivial number of the most consequential claims either rely on weak sources, outdated sources, or are not properly supported by the cited reference type. Several references are arXiv preprints or grey literature, which is not automatically disqualifying, but the manuscript uses them alongside strong novelty and engineering claims that really need higher-quality primary support. The biggest citation problem is not age; it is **misalignment** between what the paper claims and what the cited source actually supports. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn23view0turn9view1turn12view0

## Reviewer simulation and acceptance assessment

**Reviewer 1 — Technical reviewer.** *Strengths:* the manuscript is clear, the LOCO idea is directionally appropriate, and the paper honestly acknowledges that the target is deterministic from instantaneous inputs. *Weaknesses:* the regulatory grounding appears wrong, the target is a proxy rather than a measured aerodynamic mode, the seasons and car generations are mixed, and there are multiple internal inconsistencies including the Monaco speed/X-Mode contradiction and the 2026 mass mismatch. *Major concerns:* I do not trust the problem formulation as currently stated. *Minor concerns:* feature inventory is inconsistent, physics-derived features are under-justified, and terminology is not aligned with the official documents. *Questions for the authors:* What authoritative source defines the exact 240 km/h and gear ≥ 6 rule? Why does Monaco have nonzero X-Mode if the manuscript later says its maximum speed is ~215 km/h? Why is 798 kg described as the 2026 minimum mass? *Recommendation:* **Reject**. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn23view0turn9view1turn25view3

**Reviewer 2 — Experimental reviewer.** *Strengths:* the manuscript evaluates multiple model families, reports mean and standard deviation across LOCO folds, and attempts significance analysis. *Weaknesses:* only four domains are used; the tuning protocol is not properly nested; tabular baselines such as boosted trees are missing; domain-generalization baselines are missing; overlap leakage is very likely from frame-level and window-level splitting; decision-threshold, grouping, and normalization details are absent; and no control-oriented metrics such as early/late activation error, calibration, or actuation cost are reported. *Major concerns:* the benchmark is incomplete and likely optimistic. *Minor concerns:* Phase 1 feels orthogonal and bloats the paper. *Questions for the authors:* Were training and validation windows grouped by lap or contiguous segment? Why was Monza used for global tuning if it is also a LOCO test domain? Which drivers and teams were used per circuit? *Recommendation:* **Reject**. fileciteturn0file0 citeturn21academia0turn18academia0turn19academia0

**Reviewer 3 — Writing and presentation reviewer.** *Strengths:* the prose is strong, the structure is easy to follow, and many tables are clearly organized. *Weaknesses:* the title and abstract overclaim; several statements are stronger than the evidence warrants; some terminology is inconsistent with current official wording; and the figures are not fully IEEE-ready. *Major concerns:* the manuscript sounds more certain than the data justify, especially around novelty, irreducibility, deployment-readiness, and the GRU failure mechanism. *Minor concerns:* dark-theme visuals, color-dependent highlighting, and a few technical phrasing errors such as “perfect linear separability” for a random forest. *Questions for the authors:* Will you release code and data-processing scripts?
